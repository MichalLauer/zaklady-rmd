Spurious regression and trends in time series

Letâs discuss one of the challenges we will encounter when working with time series data: spurious regression. This does not imply that spurious regression is not a concern in cross-sectional analysis; rather, it is more frequently encountered in time series analysis. To illustrate the concept of spurious regression or correlation, letâs take a look at some amusing examples.

Example 1: Probability of prepayments and wild game

This example is based on a real story from a Czech bank where I worked as a consultant. There was a humorous incident where a modeler attempted to predict the probability of default on leases by using brute force regression on all variables in the database against the dependent variable. Surprisingly, one of the strongest predictors turned out to be a Japanese interest rate.

When I was building a model for predicting prepayments, I decided to humorously replicate the approach by regressing all available variables against my dependent variable. The unexpected result was that the number of wild game in Czechia emerged as a significant predictor!

In practice, rather than detrending all variables, itâs easier to add time among explanatory variables. The only drawback is that the R-squared reported after this regression is largely unusable. In our last model, it is 0.95; however, most of the explained variation in prepayments can be attributed to time, which is something we do not like to see included in R-squared. What we normally do is re-run te same regression, but with a detrended dependent variable. This means that we only have to detrend the dependent variable and leave all other variables as they are. (Here, we only have one explanatory variable, of course.)

m5 <- lm(prep.detrended ~ game + trend, data = dataS) # Same results for game, better R-squared.
stargazer(m3, m4, m5, type="text", digits=6) # Only last three models (not to exceed page width)
